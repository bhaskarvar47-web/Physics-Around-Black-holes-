When two orbiting black holes merge, a mssive bodies disturb the space time fabric around them, sending ripples of gravitational waves radiaiting outwards. These waves are predicted by Einsteins's Theory of General Relativity.

The first direct detection of gravitational waves from merging black holes was made by the LIGO (Laser Interferometer Gravitational0-Wave Observatory) in 2015. This discovery provided strong evidence for the exsistence of black holes and confirmed a major prediction of General relativity. The merging of black holes is a cataclysmic event that releases an enormous amount of energy in the form of gravitational waves, which can be detected by the sensitive instruments of LIGO and other gravitational wave observatories. These observations have opened up a new era in astrophysics, allowing scientists to study the properties of bllack hole mergers and the nature of gravity in extreme conditions.


INTRODUCTION

In 1916, Albert Einstein published the Theory of General Relativity which pridicted the existence of blackholes as a consequence of the warping of spacetime by mass and energy. However, it wasn't until the 1960s that the first theoretical models of black holes were developed,and it wasn't until the 1970s that the first observational evidence for blackholes was found. Since then, our understanding of blackholes has continued to evolve, with the new discoveries and observation providing insights into the nature of these enigmatic objectsand their role in the universe. Today, black holes are considered to be one of the most fascinating and mysterious objects in the cosmos, and they continue to be a subject of intense research and study in astrophysics and cosmology.

The discovery of binary pulsar system PSR B1913+16 by Hulse and Taylor and subsequent observations of its energy loss due to gravitational wave emission provided indirect evidence for the exisstence of gravitational waves and the merging of black holes. This discovery along with emerging astrophysical understanding, led to the recognition that direct obsevations of the amplitude and phase of gravitational waves would enable studies of additional relativistic systems and provide new tests of general relativity, especially in the dynamic strong-field regime. This motivated the development of the Laser Interferometer Gravitational-Wave Observatory (LIGO) and other gravitational wave detectors, which have since made groundbreaking discoveries in the field of astrphysics and cosmology, including the first direct detection of gravitatinal waves from merging black holes in 2015.
